# The Future of AI in Software Development

AI coding tools are no longer experimental toys. GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Claude are in daily use across engineering teams of every size. The question has shifted from "will AI change software development?" to "how fast, and what stays the same?"

I've been using these tools intensively for the past year, and I'm still forming my views. What follows is a work-in-progress — notes and observations I'm refining into something more coherent.

## What's Actually Changing

The productivity gains on well-defined, scoped tasks are real. Boilerplate, test generation, documentation, refactoring — AI handles these faster than most humans. The question is whether this compounds into something bigger.

My current hypothesis: AI is collapsing the cost of **execution** while leaving **design** largely unchanged. Writing the code is cheaper. Knowing *what* to write — the architecture, the trade-offs, the product sense — still requires human judgment.

## The Unbundling of the "Senior Engineer"

For a long time, the senior software engineer role bundled together several skills:

- Technical depth (knowing how things work)
- Pattern recognition (having seen this before)
- Execution speed (writing good code quickly)
- System thinking (seeing how pieces fit)
- Communication (translating between tech and business)

AI tools are eating the middle of this list. Technical depth and execution speed are being partially commoditized. What remains — and what becomes more valuable — is the judgment about *what* to build and the ability to communicate clearly.

## What I'm Still Figuring Out

- How do you maintain deep technical intuition when AI generates most of the code?
- What does "code review" mean when you didn't write the code?
- How do junior engineers develop expertise when the path is different?

I don't have clean answers yet. I'll update this post as my thinking develops.

## A Note on Hype

The discourse around AI in software oscillates between utopian ("engineers will be replaced in 2 years") and dismissive ("it's just autocomplete"). Reality is messier and more interesting than either pole.

The tools are genuinely useful *and* genuinely limited. Understanding both is what lets you deploy them well.
